What makes this shot worth 0.36 of a goal?

Every shot on this site gets a number — its chance of becoming a goal. This page shows how that number is made, following one real shot from start to finish. No maths or coding needed — the deep detail is folded away, and you can tap it open if you're curious.

the whole idea in 20 seconds
  • xG is a score from 0 to 1 for every shot: its chance of going in. 0.36 means roughly 1 goal in every 3 tries.
  • The model learned that from 77,627 real shots — what scored, what didn't, and why.
  • xA does the same for the pass before the shot: how likely it was to create a goal.

xG answers one narrow question: if a striker got this exact shot, from this exact spot, struck this exact way, a hundred times — how many would go in? Nothing more. It isn't a verdict on the player, and it isn't the score. It's a probability, built from everything about the shot that's known to move that probability, checked against thousands of real shots that actually happened.

01 the raw event

A shot is just three numbers when it happens

The live data feed records every touch of the ball as a dot on a grid. The model first turns that dot into two real-world facts: how far from goal, and how much of the goal the shooter could see. Our example: a left-footed shot from the edge of the box, set up by a cutback cross, and tagged by a human analyst as a big chance — the shooter had time and space to pick a spot. That one tag ends up moving the final number more than almost anything else.

04 calibration

Stop trusting the model's confidence — check its receipts

Models, like people, can be over-confident. So every prediction is checked against reality: on thousands of shots the model had never seen, did the ones it called “0.30” actually go in 30% of the time? Wherever the answer was no, the number gets corrected. The curve below is that correction, built entirely from real results.

Diagonal dashed line = "perfectly honest" (predicted probability equals actual rate). Where the curve sits above the diagonal, the raw model was underselling those shots; below it, overselling them. Our example's raw 0.300 nudges to 0.307 here — already close to honest.

05 adjusted for competition, then capped

The same shot doesn't mean the same thing everywhere

The same shot scores at slightly different rates in different competitions — different defending, goalkeeping and squad depth. So each competition gets one small nudge up or down, sized so the model's totals match that competition's real goals.

One last step: the number is never allowed below 0.002 or above 0.97 — no shot is ever a certainty, and none is truly impossible.

why it's built this way

The decisions that keep it honest

Five deliberate choices stop this model from flattering itself — from training on real, unbalanced data to grading itself only on shots it has never seen.

Read the five decisions for the curious
01
The training data isn't rebalanced.

Only about 1 shot in 10 is a goal. It's tempting to artificially even that out during training — but doing so shifts what "0.30" even means, and quietly inflates every prediction. The model trains on the real 10%, exactly as it occurs.

02
Calibration is checked on shots the model never saw.

Calibrating a model against its own training predictions is how false confidence survives review. Every calibration number on this page comes from shots held out of that fold during training, never both trained on and graded with.

03
The tree model is fenced in.

Left alone, a tree-based model can learn strange, noisy "pockets" — a fluke cluster of long-range goals it starts to treat as a rule. Monotonic constraints make that mathematically impossible: further from goal never scores higher, a wider open angle never scores lower.

04
The market shapes the curve; the goals set the level.

A public xG provider's numbers help teach the model what a well-calibrated curve looks like — but never as an input, since that number doesn't exist for a shot that hasn't been taken yet. And checked against real history, that provider's own totals run 9–11% above actual goals scored, every league, every season checked. This model's totals land at 1.00.

05
Whole matches stay together during testing.

Shots from the same game share context — the same goalkeeper, the same scoreline pressure, the same rebound chances. Splitting one match's shots across training and testing would let the model quietly peek at its own answers.

What those three scores mean plain-words glossary
Brier score — how far the model's predictions land from what actually happened, averaged over every shot in the test set (0 = perfect, 1 = worst possible). Since goals only happen on about 1 shot in 10, a model that just guessed "10%, every single time" would already score around 0.09 without knowing anything about any individual shot. This model scores lower than that — 0.0722 — meaning it's genuinely telling good chances from bad ones, not just repeating the average.
AUC — pick one random shot that scored and one random shot that didn't: this is how often the model correctly rates the goal as the more likely of the two. 0.5 would mean a coin flip (no better than guessing); 1.0 would mean it's never wrong. 0.805 means it gets that ranking right about 80.5% of the time.
ΣxG / goals — add up the predicted xG of every shot in the test set, then divide by how many of those shots actually went in. A perfect 1.00 means the model's totals aren't systematically inflated or deflated across the board — even though any single shot's number is still just a probability, not a promise. (A popular public xG provider runs 9–11% high on this same check, every league, every season — see principle 04 above.)

The same scorer feeds the PNG match reports and the live dashboard — one model, never two competing implementations, so the numbers always agree.
